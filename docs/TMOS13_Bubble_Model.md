# TMOS13 — The Bubble Model
## A Fractal Theory of Protocol, Experience, and the Structure of the Pack Web

**Working Paper — March 2026**

Robert C. Ventura
Founder & CEO, TMOS13, LLC

## Abstract

The graph metaphor — nodes, edges, paths — accurately describes the pack web's topology but misses its phenomenology. What the pack web actually looks like, from the inside, is foam. The large language model is not a network of nodes. It is a surface — undifferentiated, capable of anything, meaning nothing in particular. A pack draws a membrane around a region of that surface. Inside the membrane is a governed experience. Outside it is open text. The membrane is the protocol. The bubble is the session. The foam is the web. This paper develops the bubble model as a complementary visual theory to the graph model — one that captures the fractal, self-organizing, surface-tension properties that the graph model cannot express.


## I. The Surface

Start with the model. Not as a network, not as a graph, not as an architecture diagram. As a surface.

A large language model, viewed from above, is an undifferentiated field of latent capability. Every professional interaction that has ever been written down is in there somewhere — every intake question a doctor learned to ask, every deal structure a finance attorney has negotiated, every screening criterion an HR director developed over twenty years. The capability exists. It is not organized. It is not governed. It is not pointed at anything in particular.

This is the water. The LLM is not the bubbles. The LLM is the water.

A capable model given to a non-expert user without structure produces output that is inconsistent, ungoverned, and unauditable. The model can do almost anything. It is rarely clear what it should do.

The surface metaphor captures something the graph metaphor cannot: the model is continuous. There are no hard boundaries between what it can and cannot do. There are no nodes. There is only latent capability extending in every direction, waiting for something to give it shape.

The pack gives it shape. Not by modifying the surface — not by fine-tuning, not by weight adjustment, not by any permanent alteration of the underlying material. By drawing a membrane around a region of it.


## II. The Membrane

A pack is a membrane. It encloses a region of the model's capability space and declares: inside here, these are the rules. Inside here, these are the authorized questions. Inside here, this is what completion looks like. Inside here, this is what gets produced when the session ends.

The membrane does not change what the water is. It changes what the water does in this region. The model inside a legal intake session is the same model that exists outside it — but inside the membrane it asks intake questions, routes based on matter type, detects urgency, and produces a case file. Outside the membrane it is open text again.

The protocol is the membrane. The session is the bubble. The experience is what happens inside.

This is why protocol-native governance is categorically different from governance bolted onto an agent framework. Bolt-on governance adds rules to a system that already has behavior. Protocol-native governance defines behavior through the membrane itself. There is no ungoverned version underneath. The membrane and the behavior are the same thing.

It is also why packs are readable. A membrane is visible. You can trace its edge. You can hand the manifest to a compliance officer and ask them to read it. The membrane is the manifest. The manifest is the membrane. Try tracing the edge of a fine-tuned weight.


## III. The Fractal

A session bubble is not a simple sphere. It is fractal. Nested membranes all the way down.

The session is a bubble. Inside the session, each exchange is a smaller bubble — governed by the session's routing rules, bounded by the session's completion criteria, but also shaped by the specific exchange state at that moment in the conversation. Inside each exchange, each field being captured is a smaller bubble still — a region of the model's capability focused to a single structured output: a name, a date, a matter type, a confidence score.

The foam photograph makes this visible. The large bubbles contain smaller bubbles which contain smaller bubbles still. The structure is self-similar at every scale. A legal intake session looks, at the macro level, like a bubble. At the exchange level, like foam. At the field level, like the finest bubbles at the edge of the foam — tiny, precise, perfectly formed.

The fractal is not a metaphor. It is the actual structure of a governed session. Protocol all the way down.

What gives the fractal its shape is not design at each level. It is the surface tension — the protocol — operating consistently at every scale. The same principle that governs what the session is authorized to do also governs what the exchange is authorized to ask and what the field is authorized to capture. One membrane principle, expressed at every level of resolution.


## IV. The Foam

A single bubble floating in open water is fragile and isolated. Foam is neither.

When bubbles cluster — when packs connect into a pack web — they share surface tension at their boundaries. Adjacent bubbles influence each other's shape without merging. The legal intake bubble touches the conflict check bubble at a shared membrane. The conflict check bubble touches the engagement intake bubble. The foam holds its shape as a collective structure even as individual bubbles are added, removed, or reshaped.

This is the pack web viewed through the bubble model. Not a graph of nodes and edges — a foam of membranes sharing surface tension. The Vault is the surface tension. It is the force that makes adjacent bubbles aware of each other. It is why the conflict check session does not re-ask for the client's name — the membrane of the previous bubble touched the membrane of this one, and the surface tension carried the state forward.

The Vault is not storage. It is surface tension. The force that lets adjacent bubbles share state without merging.

Foam has a property that graphs do not: it self-organizes. You do not design where the bubbles touch. Surface tension does that. Bubbles cluster toward minimum energy configurations — the most stable arrangement given the current state of the foam. Add a new bubble and the foam reorganizes around it. Remove a bubble and the foam closes the gap. The web routes around absence. The web integrates new nodes.

No bubble is load-bearing alone. The foam as a whole holds its shape.


## V. What the Model Cannot See

Here is the thing about a bubble that the water cannot perceive: from inside the bubble, you cannot see the surface tension. You can only see the membrane. The model, operating inside a pack session, does not experience the web. It experiences the protocol. It does not know it is one bubble among thousands. It knows only what the membrane tells it to do.

This is not a limitation. It is the design. The model's job is to execute within the membrane — to ask the right questions, capture the right fields, route to the right state, produce the right deliverable. The web-level intelligence is not in the model. It is in the foam structure. In the surface tension. In the Vault carrying state from bubble to bubble.

The model is extraordinarily good at being water. That is enough. The membrane gives it purpose. The foam gives it scale. The Vault gives it memory.

You don't need a smarter model. You need a more structured surface.

NemoClaw is trying to add governance to agents that already have behavior. Moltbook let agents interact with no membrane at all — and an unsecured database let anyone commandeer any agent on the platform. OpenClaw gives users a capable agent with no membrane and calls it empowerment.

TMOS13 starts with the membrane. The bubble is not drawn around an existing agent. The bubble is the agent. Protocol native. Membrane first. Fractal by construction.


## VI. Implications

### For Governance

A membrane is auditable in a way that an agent is not. You can read the edge of a bubble. The manifest is the membrane. Any compliance officer, regulator, or domain expert can read it and understand exactly what is happening inside. This is not a feature added to the system. It is a structural property of the bubble model.

### For Scale

Foam scales differently than graphs. Adding a node to a graph requires designing the edges. Adding a bubble to foam requires only that the new bubble touch the existing foam — surface tension handles the integration. New packs join the web by virtue of their completion criteria and routing rules. The foam reorganizes. No redesign required.

### For Ownership

A bubble has an inside and an outside. Everything produced inside the membrane belongs to the membrane's author. The deliverable, the Vault artifacts, the session transcript — all of it is inside. The model is outside. The author owns the inside. The lab owns the outside. The membrane is the property boundary.

### For the AGI Question

A sufficiently dense foam — enough bubbles covering enough of the model's surface — starts to look like complete coverage. Not because any bubble is generally intelligent. Because there is no region of the surface that a bubble has not been drawn around. The intelligence is in the coverage. The foam is the AGI.


## Conclusion

The graph model tells you where the packs are and how they connect. The bubble model tells you what they are and why they hold their shape.

The LLM is the water. The pack is the membrane. The session is the bubble. The web is the foam. The Vault is the surface tension. The deliverable is what crystallizes inside when the bubble closes.

Draw enough bubbles and the water disappears. What remains is structure. What remains is the web. What remains is the thing that didn't exist before you drew the first membrane.
