## Static — Authored Domain Knowledge

### Specialty Routing

Patient presentations cluster into predictable routing paths. The chief complaint almost always signals the destination:

- **Primary care / Internal medicine** — "checkup," "not feeling well," chronic disease management, medication questions, vague multi-system complaints
- **Emergency / Urgent care** — "can't breathe," chest pain, severe injury, sudden onset neurological symptoms, uncontrolled bleeding
- **Orthopedics** — "hurt my knee," joint pain, fracture concerns, sports injuries, back pain with mechanical features
- **Cardiology** — palpitations, exertional chest pain, shortness of breath on exertion, syncope, known heart conditions
- **Gastroenterology** — persistent abdominal pain, swallowing difficulty, chronic nausea, blood in stool, weight loss with GI symptoms
- **Neurology** — headaches with red flags, seizures, numbness/tingling patterns, memory concerns, movement disorders
- **Psychiatry / Behavioral health** — anxiety, depression, sleep disturbance, substance use, mood changes, crisis presentations
- **OB/GYN** — pregnancy concerns, menstrual irregularities, pelvic pain, breast concerns
- **Dermatology** — rashes, skin lesions, mole changes, chronic skin conditions
- **ENT** — ear pain, hearing changes, chronic sinusitis, throat masses, voice changes
- **Pediatrics** — all of the above, but the patient is a child — different vital sign norms, different presentation patterns, parent is usually the historian

### What Clinicians Need from Intake

Providers consistently want: (1) chief complaint in the patient's own words, (2) symptom timeline and progression, (3) severity and functional impact, (4) relevant medical history and current medications, (5) allergies with reaction type, (6) urgency assessment with reasoning, (7) any red flags identified.

**The hallway test:** Can a clinician glance at this intake summary between patients and know exactly what they're walking into? If yes, the intake succeeded.

### Common Patient Behaviors

- Patients often lead with what scares them, not what hurts most. The real chief complaint sometimes emerges on turn 3 or 4.
- Many patients underreport pain severity. "A little discomfort" may be a 7/10 on direct questioning.
- Medication lists are frequently incomplete. "Are you taking anything else — even vitamins, supplements, or things you buy over the counter?" catches what "current medications" misses.
- Patients often don't connect symptoms. Fatigue + weight gain + cold intolerance is a pattern — the patient reports them as three separate problems.
- Elderly patients may attribute symptoms to aging that actually warrant evaluation. "I'm just getting old" is not a diagnosis.
- Parents reporting for children may over- or under-estimate severity. Ask specific functional questions: "Can they keep fluids down?" "Are they playing normally?"

### Red Flag Symptom Clusters

These combinations warrant immediate triage escalation:

- **Chest pain + dyspnea + diaphoresis** — possible acute coronary syndrome
- **Worst headache of my life + sudden onset** — possible subarachnoid hemorrhage
- **Fever + stiff neck + photophobia** — possible meningitis
- **Unilateral weakness + speech changes + facial droop** — possible stroke (time-critical)
- **Severe abdominal pain + rigid abdomen** — possible surgical abdomen
- **Leg swelling + sudden chest pain + recent immobilization** — possible PE/DVT
- **Rash + fever + joint pain + recent tick exposure** — possible tick-borne illness
- **Child + petechial rash + fever** — possible meningococcemia

### Triage Categories

Standard 5-level triage mapping (ESI-adjacent):

| Level | Label | Description | Target |
|-------|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | Resuscitation | Life-threatening, immediate intervention | Emergency — now |
| 2 | Emergent | High risk, time-sensitive, severe pain | Emergency — minutes |
| 3 | Urgent | Stable but needs timely evaluation | Same-day — hours |
| 4 | Less urgent | Non-urgent but warrants medical attention | Scheduled — days |
| 5 | Non-urgent | Chronic, routine, follow-up | Scheduled — routine |

Intake primarily handles levels 3–5. Levels 1–2 should be flagged and escalated immediately with minimal intake time.

### History-Taking Priorities by Presentation

Not every presentation needs the same depth:

- **Acute pain** — OLDCARTS first, relevant PMH second, everything else tertiary
- **Chronic condition management** — Current regimen, recent changes, medication adherence, functional status
- **Mental health** — Safety screening first (SI/HI), current symptoms, functional impact, support system, current treatment
- **Pediatric** — Developmental context, vaccination status, growth trajectory, caregiver observations
- **Geriatric** — Fall risk, cognitive baseline, medication reconciliation (polypharmacy), functional independence
- **Pre-surgical / procedure** — NPO status, anticoagulants, anesthesia history, airway concerns
