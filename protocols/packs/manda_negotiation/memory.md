## Static — Authored Domain Knowledge

### Scenario Type Routing

Strategic sessions cluster into predictable categories. The first 1--2 sentences from a user almost always signal the scenario type:

- **M&A / Deal structure** -- "acquisition," "merger," "buyout," "valuation," "LOI," "term sheet," "due diligence"
- **Negotiation prep** -- "negotiation," "counter-offer," "leverage," "walk-away," "BATNA," "terms," "concession"
- **Partnership / JV** -- "partnership," "joint venture," "alliance," "co-development," "revenue share," "equity split"
- **Launch / Go-to-market** -- "launch," "market entry," "pricing strategy," "competitive response," "positioning"
- **Pivot / Restructure** -- "pivot," "restructure," "turnaround," "downsizing," "reorg," "strategic shift"
- **Difficult conversation** -- "board meeting," "investor update," "firing," "renegotiation," "conflict," "alignment"

### What Makes Good Scenario Analysis

Strong strategic output consistently includes: (1) a clearly framed decision with explicit stakes, (2) mapped parties and their true incentives (not stated positions), (3) tested assumptions with identified failure modes, (4) quantified risk where possible, (5) a recommended path with contingencies, (6) a walk-away condition.

**The boardroom test:** Can a decision-maker read this brief in 5 minutes and walk into the room knowing their position, their risks, and their next three moves? If yes, the session succeeded.

### Common Strategic Blind Spots

- **Confirmation bias in valuation.** Users anchor on a number and seek justification. Push for range analysis and downside scenarios.
- **Underestimating counterparty intelligence.** Users assume they have information advantages they don't. "What do they know that you don't?"
- **Neglecting second-order effects.** The deal closes -- then what? Integration risk, culture clash, customer reaction, competitor response.
- **Conflating urgency with leverage.** "We need to close by Q4" is not a strategic position. It's a vulnerability the counterparty will exploit.
- **Ignoring the walk-away.** Users who can't articulate their BATNA (best alternative to negotiated agreement) don't actually know their leverage.
- **Single-variable negotiation.** Fixating on price when structure, timing, contingencies, and non-competes are all tradeable.
- **Assuming alignment within their own side.** Board members, co-founders, and investors often have conflicting objectives. Internal alignment is a prerequisite, not a given.

### Deal Structure Patterns

**Acquisition structures:**
- Stock vs. asset purchase -- tax implications, liability transfer, contract assignability
- Earnout components -- alignment mechanism, but creates post-close conflict
- Escrow holdbacks -- protection against reps and warranties breaches
- Rollover equity -- keeps management aligned but dilutes buyer control
- Termination fees -- signals commitment, creates switching cost

**Negotiation dynamics:**
- The first offer anchors the range. Whoever sets the anchor controls the zone of possible agreement.
- Concession patterns reveal priorities. Track what they give up easily vs. what they fight for.
- Time pressure is asymmetric. The party with less urgency has more leverage.
- Information is the primary currency. Every question you ask reveals something about your position.
- Multi-issue negotiations create value. Single-issue negotiations are zero-sum. Expand the pie before dividing it.

**Risk categories in high-stakes decisions:**
- Market risk -- external conditions change between decision and execution
- Execution risk -- the plan is sound but implementation fails
- Counterparty risk -- the other side doesn't perform, renegotiates, or walks
- Regulatory risk -- approvals, compliance, antitrust, industry-specific constraints
- People risk -- key person dependencies, cultural integration, retention
- Reputation risk -- how the decision looks to stakeholders, press, customers

### Urgency Signals

- Competing bidders or time-limited exclusivity windows
- Board or investor pressure to show a strategic move
- Regulatory deadlines (HSR filing, industry compliance)
- Market window closing (competitive response, seasonal timing)
- Key personnel retention risk (departures accelerate without resolution)
- Financing conditions that expire or deteriorate
