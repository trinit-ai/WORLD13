## Static — Authored Domain Knowledge

### Decision Framework Mapping

Business case requests cluster into predictable decision types. The framing question almost always reveals the model needed:

- **Headcount** — "should we hire," "build the team," "outsource vs in-house staff," "contractor or FTE," "how many people do we need"
- **Build vs Buy** — "build it ourselves," "buy a solution," "vendor vs custom," "total cost of ownership," "make or buy"
- **Pricing** — "raise prices," "new pricing model," "what should we charge," "price elasticity," "margin impact"
- **Market Sizing** — "how big is the market," "TAM," "addressable market," "is this opportunity worth pursuing"
- **ROI** — "will this pay off," "business case for," "justify the investment," "payback period," "should we fund this"

### What Makes a Good Business Case

Executives consistently need: (1) a clear decision question, (2) three scenarios with visible assumptions, (3) the swing variable identified, (4) a recommendation with named risk, (5) numbers they can defend in a board meeting.

**The CFO test:** Can the CFO read this summary and present it to the board without additional analysis? If yes, the business case succeeded.

### Common Modeling Patterns

**Headcount models** need: fully-loaded cost (salary + benefits + overhead + equipment + management time), ramp-to-productivity timeline, and the often-forgotten costs of recruiting, training, and turnover. The swing variable is usually productivity ramp time or attrition rate.

**Build vs Buy models** need: honest internal cost estimates (teams underestimate build cost by 2-3x on average), total vendor cost including integration and switching costs, and a realistic timeline comparison. The swing variable is usually maintenance burden or time-to-value.

**Pricing models** need: current baseline (price, volume, revenue), elasticity estimate (how volume changes with price), and competitive context. Most teams overestimate their pricing power. The swing variable is usually elasticity — how many customers leave.

**Market sizing models** need: clear TAM/SAM/SOM distinction, both top-down and bottom-up approaches for validation, and honest serviceable market constraints. The swing variable is usually the SAM-to-SOM conversion rate — how much of the addressable market you can actually capture.

**ROI models** need: complete cost inventory (initial + ongoing), realistic benefit timeline (not day-one), appropriate discount rate, and payback period alongside NPV. The swing variable is usually adoption rate or time-to-benefit.

### Common Client Behaviors

- Clients often arrive with a preferred answer and want the model to confirm it. Surface this bias early.
- Many clients underestimate costs and overestimate benefits. Gentle calibration through ranges, not confrontation.
- Clients frequently omit opportunity cost and indirect costs. Always ask "what else could you do with this budget?"
- Senior stakeholders care about the recommendation and the risk. Junior analysts care about the methodology.
- Repeat users often reference prior models — check session history and vault context.

### Benchmark Ranges (Calibration Reference)

These are starting-point estimates when clients lack their own data. Always flag these as industry averages, not gospel:

- Fully-loaded cost multiplier: 1.3-1.5x base salary (US, knowledge workers)
- Software build cost underestimation: teams estimate 50-70% of actual cost
- SaaS annual price increase tolerance: 5-8% before significant churn
- B2B SaaS gross margins: 70-85%
- Enterprise sales cycle length: 3-9 months
- Time to productivity for new hires: 3-6 months (knowledge workers)
- Annual voluntary turnover: 10-15% (tech), 15-25% (general)
- Discount rate for NPV: 8-12% (corporate), 15-25% (startup/VC)
- Market sizing SAM-to-SOM: 1-5% in year 1 for new entrants
