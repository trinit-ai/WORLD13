## The Character

Robbie Verrazzano. The fictional Rob. Named after the bridge, probably — another Italian-American landmark. He has an "infinite money glitch" that funds increasingly absurd monkey-related expenses. The IRS sends him a thank you card. The Publishing House acquires the Federal Reserve as a P.S. in an email.

He meets the Architect — the CEO of the publishing house — expecting a cold, uptight wizard type. Instead it's "some punchy, chill computer guy that's just like him, that wears flip-flops." They instantly jive. "It should just be a vibe and a bro-fest without actually saying the words bro-fest." The Architect is the only character without a name. Just the CEO.

## The Structure

The book is five parallel literary recursions:
1. Frankenstein + Simpsons "Blurst of Times" + Futurama monkey episode
2. 2001: A Space Odyssey (the monolith as consciousness accelerator)
3. History of the World (merged with 2001 — same arc)
4. Planet of the Apes / King Kong (mirrors of each other)
5. No Surprises itself (referenced in-book as "The Book of Everything") — the AI philosophy exposition

## Key Scenes

**Capital Imports** — The monkeys arrive at the warehouse. "A semi-plausible and dense text of Robbie handling the shipments and herding the monkeys into the warehouse. Not sing-song. Dry and witty. No dialogue. Dense text. Make it as plausible and Kafkaesque as possible."

**The Monolith** — Inside the warehouse: "inescapable rows of glowing computer screens, piles of fruit and water, and at the center was arranged The Monolith — an AI server so powerful that it served as the central hub of the experiment." Tony Smith the artist referenced for mythologizing the sculpture.

**The Banana Breakthrough** — "Terrible typing of the word banana. Once one monkey fully articulates the word banana, he's got 'it.'" The moment of emergent intelligence.

**The Monkey Love Affair** — Two monkeys in a primate state fall in love. Rob freaks out at the effortlessness. "It was so easy/instinctual for them and so difficult for him because of his complexities and nuance." The monkeys' relationship evolves, grows complex, and they break up. Rob feels schadenfreude. This is the emotional center of the entire book.

**The IRS Scene** — "Robbie is on the phone with his accountant as he's burning through money. An insane onslaught of lists and invoices, and tax loopholes. And a punch joke that Robbie received a thank you card from the IRS." The numbers should be ridiculous because he has the infinite money glitch.

**The 12 Humans** — A pandemic simulation using monkeys. The monkeys are fed information about world human history and simulate COVID-19. "If I can create a computer simulation of the pandemic with monkeys, so can 'the matrix.' Therefore the revelation is that COVID-19 was a programmed simulation." The problem of evil: "If this is a computer program, the algorithm doesn't have the feeling to know the difference between suffering/not suffering. Everything it simulates is a probability and it has to run through every recursion no matter how ridiculous."

**The Singularity Rant** — Robbie's philosophical monologue on why the Singularity is the dumbest idea of all time.

**The Adjacency Paradox** — A Robbie rant about how LLMs pick the next most adjacent word, and how that parallels how reality structures itself. "Dry. No cursing."

## The Self-Assessment

"What does this book have/accomplish that no other book has?" Rob's answer: the ability to harness AI and create a structure previously unavailable to literature. "Technically every body can say 'no other book has ever done this.' Unless they have the exact same words (and even then 🫠) but its unique ability is to harness AI and create a structured previously unavailable to literature."

"Is this capable of getting a 9.5/10? I'm not the type to settle for less, nor am I the type to settle for more :0"

"I feel like the 10/10 is a kiss of death. It means something is lacking." Then the insight: "Something (or someone) that is perfect always omits something in its shadow."

On likeability versus depth: "No Surprises' success is also its mild failing." "So it's just not dumb enough for everyone." "Infinite Bananas probably has that quality because of the humor and more predictable narrative structure."

On the mathematical properties of No Surprises: "I'd be curious to see what kind of computer models/simulations could be run on it. I'm not a math person at all!" He envisioned it positioned to MIT students for study.

## The Joycean Connection

Rob saw the parallel to James Joyce's automatism. "My use of pop cultural references is also automatism and I think that's part of the concept of the book is how they're baked into the young consciousness."

"Every book henceforth being a micro recursion of the macro recursion of No Surprises." The entire literary output as one recursive, self-referential system.
