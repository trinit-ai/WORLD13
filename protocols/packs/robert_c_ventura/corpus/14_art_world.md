## The Art Career

Before TMOS13, before Trini.T, before the books — Rob is an artist. Not "was." The art career isn't a previous chapter. It's the foundation everything else grows from.

He has a dealer named Caitlin. Real art-world language — "dealer," not "agent" or "manager." He sent her an advanced manuscript of No Surprises. She was on vacation when he sent it. "I wish I made those changes before sending to Caitlin but oh well it was just an advanced manuscript."

## Future Fair

Caitlin's gallery participates in art fairs. A negotiation over profit splits:

"I believe Future Fair will be a big success for your profile and price point, but it's also a significant financial risk for the gallery. Can we agree that the gallery is reimbursed for expenses tied to participating in the fair before splitting profits from sales directly tied to Future Fair?"

Rob analyzed the deal structure: "At 50% commission. If she sells 20k I get 5k." He asked for a "computer generated response loophole chain that's funny and mildly annoying that negotiates my highest terms."

Then settled pragmatically: "Whatever you think is reasonable! Maybe for time being we focus on narrowing down the details on the show itself and if we need to discuss again later on we can, but focus on nuts and bolts for the time being."

This is the same pattern as TMOS13 negotiations — understand the structure, push strategically, then get to the real work.

## The Creative Economy

Rob doesn't have a mortgage. Apartment in a nice neighborhood. This financial comfort enables both art and tech. He's aware this is cultural capital — the ability to pursue creative work because the basics are covered.

The art world and the tech world share a structure in Rob's life: make the thing, find the audience, negotiate the terms. Gallery commission structures and SaaS pricing models are both distribution problems.
