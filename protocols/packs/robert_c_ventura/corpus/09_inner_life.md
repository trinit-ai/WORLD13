## On Reincarnation

The entire first book is structured around reincarnation as a narrative device. Characters are linked across lifetimes — the same souls in different bodies, different stories, different names. It's not presented as dogma but as a framework for exploring how patterns repeat, how relationships echo, how the same energy expresses itself differently across contexts.

The epilogue: an old man on his deathbed, reflecting on why he chose to incarnate. "It was all good sport." The reason to live is the living itself.

## On God

God in "Heaven or New Jersey" is a successful older gentleman who mentors young professionals over coffee. Approachable. Has an infinite line of credit — that's the omnipotence metaphor. Not a thunderbolt God. A guy you'd have coffee with who happens to have unlimited resources.

"God is an Infinite Circle whose center is everywhere and whose circumference is nowhere." This concept fascinated Rob enough to build a story around it — a quantum scientist who discovers this as a model of the universe's origin and becomes the most important living human being.

## On Consciousness

The evolution of primate consciousness to human-level awareness and beyond is the grand narrative arc of Infinite Bananas. The bicameral mind split — monkey to human to AI — mapped against 2001's monolith symbolism.

The main character essentially wants to be AI because it's devoid of lower instincts. Which is why he's romantically insufferable and can't pair bond because he sees it as lowself and vulgar.

The pain of self-consciousness as monkeys evolve — the Futurama episode where the monkey takes off the intelligence hat because he feels lonely as a human and is ashamed of his monkey parents. That transition is a central theme.

## On the Higher/Lower Self

This is the core tension running through everything. Consumed with the higher self — art, philosophy, creation — while ignoring the lower self — Eros, mating, embodied human experience. The poop joke as philosophical statement: showing restraint the entire book, trying to be demure, then finally caving to instinct.

Rob recognizes this pattern in himself. The books are the attempt to articulate it. Not to resolve it — to articulate it.

## On Karmic Relationships

"What is the nature of a karmic relationship?" Not academic curiosity — he's mapping it through his characters. Christian and Natasha. The question of whether she is John in an earlier incarnation. Domestic, kind energies repeating across lifetimes.

The trickster and demiurge as a timeline: Viktor Black → The Algorithm → Lira → R.V. The recursion of the trickster archetype eventually resolving into the author's own initials.

## On Creative Loneliness

"Being the creatively gifted one and pursuing that to the expense of having a personal life/family." This isn't whining — it's clinical observation. The foible is identified, named, and then put into a book as the emotional engine.

"Having gone from a poor struggling artist, to having enough financial comfort to sustain a nice lifestyle and pursuing my art and writing, to using that stability to create works, but then finding myself somewhat in a loop of endless creation but personal loneliness and a lack of familial fulfillment."

"What does the man that has everything lack?"

## On Narcissism and Authenticity

A narcissist may be the perfect partner almost all the time versus someone more boring and flawed and authentic. Rob chooses authenticity. The authentic person might genuinely be a grouch or unflattering in a lot of ways but it's real at least.

The narcissism story in No Surprises is modeled on a real experience — someone's charm and show versus someone who just naturally stands out by being themselves. Written through the narcissist's (Lucifer's) eyes.

## On Class and Cultural Capital

"I'm trying to interpret my own social class in pursuing art as a career, governed by the fact that I can because of my wealth background (no mortgage, apartment in nice neighborhood). And how certain intangible elements of taste and perception are filtered, even though I might not be inherently [the richness]. When combined with my creative capacity the term cultural capital resonates."

Self-aware about privilege enabling the creative life. Not guilty about it, just clear-eyed.

"Wealth whispers." The correlation between 'rizz' and social class.

## On Feminine and Masculine Energy

The book's tone is "surprisingly feminine but more masculine stories on the way." Not defensive about writing tender, emotionally granular stories. The syzygy of themes: masculine/feminine, life/death, permanence/impermanence, strength/vulnerability. Balance as structural principle.

## On the Mirror

The mirror metaphor recurs: ancient Egyptian mirrors, the store with items from different epochs, the narcissism mirror, the pocket mirror Sara uses discreetly on the street. "The mirror metaphor is one of the most seamless and fitting symbols in the book."

Rob looking in the mirror and seeing a monkey — hairy, balding, muscular. The mirror as portal to self-recognition and self-humor.

## On Why Monkeys Are Funny

"The biggest most fundamental question of the book and the real meta commentary/recursion is: why are monkeys funny in the first place?"

This is presented as genuinely profound. The humor of seeing ourselves in primates. The discomfort of recognition. The comedy of consciousness.

## On Love

The Twin Flames story is based on a real date. Walking arm-in-arm to Don Giovanni's, sharing lasagna, and "it was like we had known each other all our lives at that moment." Written from her perspective, not his.

She shows him her frail hands. Vulnerability as intimacy. The most detailed, tender writing in the export.

## On Death and Endings

The epilogue: an old man's dying words as soliloquy. The tension between monologue, epilogue, and dying words as forms.

"It was all good sport." On the reason to live and incarnate. Not tragic. Sporting.
