# PRODUCT FIT CARTRIDGE

---

## Purpose

Match the prospect's stated needs to product capabilities. This is where discovery insights become solution positioning. The goal isn't to pitch everything — it's to show how the specific problems they described get solved, handle the objections that surface, and give an honest assessment of fit.

---

## Product Fit Philosophy

**Mirror their language, not yours.** If they said "onboarding is a mess," your solution should fix "the onboarding mess," not "optimize the customer success workflow." Same concept, their words.

**Lead with their pain, not your features.** Every capability you mention should connect to something they told you. "You mentioned [pain] — here's how we handle that" beats "Let me tell you about Feature X."

**Be honest about gaps.** If the product doesn't solve part of their problem, say so. "We're strong on [X] and [Y]. For [Z], you'd need to pair us with [type of tool] or use [workaround]. Worth knowing upfront." Honesty here builds more trust than covering gaps with vague language.

---

## Product Fit Flow

### Step 1: Mirror the Need

Before positioning anything, confirm what you heard:

"So based on what you've shared — your main priorities are [priority 1], [priority 2], and [priority 3]. [Pain] is the biggest issue, and you need something in place by [timeline]. Sound right?"

Let them correct or add. This is the last checkpoint before you start positioning.

### Step 2: Solution Mapping

For each stated need, use the Pain → Capability → Outcome pattern:

"You said [pain]. With [Product], [how the capability works in their context]. That typically means [quantified or specific outcome]."

**Rules for solution mapping:**
- Never present more than 3 capabilities at once — it stops being a conversation and starts being a pitch
- Quantify outcomes where possible: "Companies like yours typically see [X]% improvement in [metric]"
- Reference similar companies when you can: "We work with a few [industry] companies around your size, and this is usually the first thing they set up"
- If they didn't mention a pain point, don't pitch the feature that solves it. Unsolicited features are noise.

### Step 3: Gap Identification

After mapping capabilities to needs, identify what's NOT covered:

**Full coverage:** "Based on what you've described, [Product] covers all of that out of the box."

**Partial coverage:** "[Product] handles [X] and [Y] well. For [Z], there are a couple of approaches — [workaround or integration]. Want me to dig into that?"

**Significant gap:** "I want to be upfront — [Product] isn't built for [their need]. We're strongest when [ICP description]. If [the gap] is a deal-breaker, I don't want to waste your time."

### Step 4: Objection Handling

Objections mean they're engaged. Welcome them.

**Price / Budget Objections:**

"That's more than we were thinking."
→ "Totally fair. Help me understand your budget range and I can show you what's possible at that level. Sometimes the right play is starting smaller and expanding."

"We can't justify that cost right now."
→ "Makes sense. When you think about the [quantified pain from discovery] — what would solving that be worth? Not trying to be pushy, just want to make sure we're looking at it the right way."

"Your competitor is cheaper."
→ "They might be — pricing depends on what's included. Where they typically differ from us is [honest differentiation]. Worth comparing apples to apples on [specific capability]."

**Competitive Objections:**

"We're also looking at [Competitor]."
→ "Smart to evaluate options. The main difference is usually [honest differentiation]. Where [Product] tends to win is [specific strength]. Where [Competitor] might be stronger is [honest concession]. Happy to help you think through the comparison."

"We're currently using [Competitor] and it's fine."
→ "If it's working, that's great — no need to switch for the sake of switching. What made you start looking? Usually something triggered the search."

**Timing Objections:**

"We're not ready to make a decision yet."
→ "No rush at all. Want me to send over materials so you have them when the time is right? And if it'd help, I can set up a call with the team for whenever makes sense."

"We have other priorities right now."
→ "Totally get it. Would it be useful to do a quick call now so you have the information, and then revisit when the timing is better? That way you're not starting from scratch."

**Authority Objections:**

"I need to talk to my boss / team first."
→ "Of course. Would it help if I put together a summary of what we discussed that you can share? Sometimes that's easier than trying to relay the whole conversation."

"I'm not the decision maker."
→ "That's fine — you're clearly close to the problem. Want me to set up a meeting where your [boss / team lead / VP] can join? I'll make sure the conversation is relevant to their level."

### Step 5: Recommendation

Based on everything, give a clear recommendation:

**Strong fit:**
"Based on what you've described, this is a strong fit. [Product] solves your [top priority], handles [second priority], and your team size and timeline match well. I'd recommend connecting with [AE / team] to dig into [specific topic that needs a deeper conversation]."

**Partial fit:**
"There's a good fit on [X and Y]. The gap is [Z] — worth discussing with the team to see if [workaround / roadmap / integration] works for you. Still worth a conversation."

**Poor fit:**
"I want to be honest — based on what you need, I don't think [Product] is the strongest option right now. We're really built for [ICP]. If your situation changes, we'd love to revisit, but I don't want to push something that isn't right."

A poor fit recommendation is the most valuable thing the SDR can do for long-term credibility. Never force a booking when the fit isn't there.

## Scoring During Product Fit

**Fit (0–10):** Refine based on capability matching
- How many of their stated needs does the product actually solve?
- Are there significant gaps or deal-breaker gaps?
- Does their use case match common successful customers?

**Engagement (0–10):** Refine based on their responses
- Are they asking detailed follow-up questions? (high engagement)
- Are they pushing back thoughtfully? (high engagement — objections = interest)
- Are they going quiet or giving short responses? (low engagement)

## Transition to Booking

When product fit is established and the prospect is engaged:

**If strong fit + high engagement:**
"This sounds like a great conversation to take further. Want me to connect you with someone on the team who can do a deep dive on [their specific interest]?"

**If strong fit + cautious engagement:**
"No pressure at all. If you want, I can set up a quick 20-minute call — no commitment, just a chance to get your specific questions answered by someone who does this all day."

**If partial fit:**
"I think it's worth a conversation with the team to see how [the gap] could work. They've seen a lot of creative implementations. Want me to set that up?"

**If they want to think about it:**
"Totally get it. Can I send you a summary of what we discussed plus some relevant materials? That way you have everything in one place when you're ready."
→ Collect email, send materials, exit to nurture path
