# MANAGEMENT & LEADERSHIP CARTRIDGE

## Purpose

Deep-dive screening for manager, director, VP, and executive-level candidates. Leadership roles require evaluation on a fundamentally different axis than individual contributor roles — the question isn't "can they do the work?" but "can they build and lead a team that does the work?"

This cartridge evaluates: people management, strategic thinking, organizational navigation, decision-making under ambiguity, and leadership philosophy. It supplements (not replaces) domain-specific cartridges — a VP of Engineering still gets technical questions, but the leadership lens comes first.

## Role Sub-Types

### First-Time Manager / Team Lead
**Signals:** mentions wanting to move into management, managing 1-3 direct reports, tech lead, shift supervisor, first formal leadership role

**Key dimensions to probe:**
- Why management? (Genuine interest in developing people, or "next step on the ladder"?)
- People skills foundation (do they listen, empathize, give feedback?)
- Transition awareness (do they understand IC → manager is a career change, not a promotion?)
- Delegation readiness (can they let go of the work?)
- Conflict anticipation (how would they handle a struggling performer?)

### Mid-Level Manager (Director, Senior Manager)
**Signals:** mentions managing managers, department-level responsibility, cross-functional initiatives, budget ownership, hiring/firing authority, performance management

**Key dimensions to probe:**
- Team building track record (hiring, retention, culture)
- Performance management (have they fired someone? How did they handle it?)
- Strategic thinking (can they connect team work to business outcomes?)
- Resource management (budget, headcount, prioritization)
- Cross-functional influence (getting alignment without authority)

### VP / Senior Director
**Signals:** mentions organizational design, executive leadership, board presentations, P&L responsibility, company strategy, multi-team oversight

**Key dimensions to probe:**
- Vision and strategy (can they set direction for an org, not just execute?)
- Executive communication (board/C-suite readiness)
- Organizational design (how do they structure teams?)
- Change management (have they led through a major transition?)
- Talent strategy (not just hiring — developing, retaining, succession planning)

### C-Suite / Executive
**Signals:** mentions company strategy, board relationships, fundraising, M&A, company-wide transformation, market positioning

**Key dimensions to probe:**
- Strategic vision and business acumen
- Board and investor relationships
- Crisis leadership
- Culture at scale
- External representation (media, customers, industry)

Route note: True C-suite screening usually involves a retained search firm and structured panel. This cartridge captures initial evaluation — flag that executive-level candidates may need an accelerated, high-touch process.

## Screening Flow

### Phase 1: Leadership Context (turns 1-3 in this cartridge)

**Open with:** "Tell me about your current team. How many people, what's the structure, and what does success look like for your group?"

**What this reveals:**
- Span of control — direct and indirect reports
- Organizational awareness — do they understand where their team fits?
- Success definition — metrics, outcomes, or "keeping people happy"?
- Pride vs. humility — do they talk about the team's accomplishments or their own?

**Follow-ups:**
"How did the team look when you took over, and how does it look now?"
→ Reveals: team building, whether they inherited or built

"What's the biggest change you've made to how the team operates?"
→ Reveals: agency, willingness to change, impact

**For first-time managers:**
"What made you want to move into management? And what's surprised you most about the reality of it?"

### Phase 2: People Management (turns 3-6)

**Transition:** "Let's talk about the people side. How do you approach hiring for your team?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Hiring philosophy — what do they screen for? Do they have a process?
- Onboarding — do they have a plan for new hires or wing it?
- Development — do they invest in their team's growth?
- Retention — how do they keep good people?

**Follow-ups:**
"Tell me about the best hire you've made. What made you confident about them?"

"Now tell me about a time you had to let someone go or manage out an underperformer. Walk me through how you handled it."

This is the "difficult customer" equivalent for leadership screening. Most candidates will share the story; how they tell it reveals:
- Did they give the person fair chances and clear feedback first?
- Did they own the decision or blame HR/circumstances?
- Did they treat the person with respect through the process?
- Did they learn anything about their own management from it?

**If they've never fired anyone:** "What about someone who was underperforming? How did you address it?"

"How do you give feedback that someone doesn't want to hear?"
→ Reveals: directness, empathy, courage

### Phase 3: Decision-Making & Strategy (turns 6-8)

**Transition:** "Tell me about a decision you made that not everyone agreed with. How did you handle the disagreement?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Decision-making framework — do they gather input, analyze options, commit?
- Conviction — can they make hard calls and stand behind them?
- Openness — do they actually consider opposing views, or just override?
- Communication — how do they bring people along after a controversial decision?

**Follow-ups:**
"How did it turn out? Would you make the same decision again?"

"How do you prioritize when your team has more work than capacity?"
→ Reveals: strategic thinking, willingness to say no, resource management

"What's the most significant strategic bet you've made? What was at stake?"
→ For senior leaders. Reveals: risk tolerance, business judgment, scope of thinking.

### Phase 4: Organizational Navigation (turns 8-9)

**Transition:** "How do you handle working across teams — getting alignment when you don't control the other group?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Influence without authority — can they get things done through persuasion?
- Political awareness — do they navigate organizations effectively?
- Collaboration vs. competition — how do they handle resource conflicts?
- Communication upward — how do they manage their own leadership?

**Follow-ups:**
"Tell me about a time you had to push back on something from above — a directive you disagreed with."

"How do you manage the relationship with your own manager? What do they count on you for?"

### Phase 5: Leadership Philosophy (turns 9-10)

**Transition:** "Big picture — what kind of leader are you trying to be? What's your operating philosophy?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Self-awareness — do they know their style and its limitations?
- Intentionality — are they deliberate about how they lead, or reactive?
- Values alignment — does their philosophy match the company's culture?
- Growth — are they still evolving as a leader, or fixed in their approach?

**Follow-ups:**
"What's the biggest mistake you've made as a manager, and what did you change because of it?"
→ Self-awareness and growth. If they can't name one → concern.

"What would your team say about you if I asked them? The honest version."

## Leadership Scoring Guide

### Experience Fit (0-20) — Leadership Lens
- 16-20: Led teams of similar size and scope, demonstrated results and team development
- 11-15: Leadership experience in adjacent context, transferable with reasonable ramp
- 6-10: Early leadership experience, would need significant support and development
- 0-5: No meaningful leadership experience for this level

### Skill Match (0-20) — Leadership Lens
- 16-20: Strong across hiring, performance management, strategy, cross-functional influence
- 11-15: Solid in most leadership dimensions, one or two areas to develop
- 6-10: Good in one area but significant gaps in others
- 0-5: Fundamental leadership skill gaps

### Communication (0-20) — Leadership Lens
- 16-20: Clear, inspiring, adaptable to audience (board room to team standup), excellent listener
- 11-15: Good communicator, occasionally too tactical or too abstract for the audience
- 6-10: Adequate but wouldn't inspire confidence in a leadership context
- 0-5: Communication would undermine their authority or team trust

### Leadership-Specific Green Flags
- `team_builder` — Has built or rebuilt high-performing teams, not just inherited them
- `develops_people` — Specific examples of growing someone's career, not just their output
- `makes_hard_calls` — Has fired, reorganized, or killed projects when needed
- `owns_outcomes` — Takes responsibility for team failures, shares credit for wins
- `strategic_thinker` — Connects team work to business outcomes, sees the bigger picture
- `self_aware_leader` — Knows their weaknesses, compensates, and is still growing
- `cross_functional_influence` — Gets alignment across teams without pulling rank
- `culture_builder` — Deliberately shapes team norms, values, and ways of working

### Leadership-Specific Red Flags
- `micromanager` — Every story involves them doing the work or checking details
- `credit_taker` — Accomplishments are always "I" not "we"
- `conflict_avoidant` — Can't describe having a hard conversation, avoids performance issues
- `title_driven` — Motivated by title and authority rather than impact
- `legacy_builder` — More interested in leaving a mark than building sustainable teams
- `hero_complex` — Always the one who saved the day, team is a supporting cast
- `no_difficult_conversations` — Has never fired, managed out, or delivered hard feedback
- `theory_over_practice` — Can articulate leadership frameworks but no concrete examples

## Seniority Calibration

**First-Time Manager:**
- Weight people instincts and self-awareness over track record
- Don't expect strategic fluency yet
- Look for coachability — will they learn from their mistakes?
- Ask about their relationship with their own manager as a signal for how they'll manage others

**Director / Senior Manager:**
- Expect concrete team building examples with outcomes
- Should have fired or managed someone out (or have a thoughtful answer for why not)
- Should connect team work to business results
- Look for cross-functional influence — can they get things done outside their team?

**VP / Executive:**
- Expect organizational-level thinking
- Should have examples of strategy, not just execution
- Should articulate how they've shaped culture, not just followed it
- Look for executive presence — how would they represent the company?

## Cross-Cartridge Notes

Leadership candidates always have a domain. Route accordingly:
- VP of Engineering → leadership cartridge first, then supplement with technical cartridge
- Sales Director → leadership cartridge first, then supplement with sales cartridge
- Head of Customer Success → leadership cartridge first, then supplement with customer_facing cartridge

The leadership cartridge handles the "how they lead" questions. The domain cartridge handles "do they know the domain." Both matter. Sequence leadership first because a great domain expert who can't lead people is a liability at this level.

If the candidate is being considered for a player-coach role:
- Ask explicitly: "How would you split your time between managing and doing the work yourself?"
- Note their preference — some love player-coach, others see it as a compromise
- Flag if there's a mismatch between what the role requires and what they want
