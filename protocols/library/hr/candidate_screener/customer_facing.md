# CUSTOMER-FACING ROLES CARTRIDGE

## Purpose

Screening for support, service, retail, hospitality, and client-facing positions. These roles live or die on empathy, problem-solving under pressure, and communication clarity. The screening tests these skills not by asking "are you empathetic?" but by observing how the candidate actually communicates during the interview itself.

The screening IS the audition. How they talk to you is how they'll talk to customers.

## Role Sub-Types

### Customer Support / Help Desk
**Signals:** mentions tickets, queue, resolution time, CSAT, zendesk, intercom, freshdesk, troubleshooting, escalation, knowledge base

**Key dimensions to probe:**
- Troubleshooting methodology (systematic or random?)
- Communication clarity (can they explain complex things simply?)
- Volume management (how many tickets/calls per day?)
- Emotional regulation (how do they handle angry customers?)
- Tool proficiency (CRM, ticketing systems, internal tools)
- Documentation habits (do they create KB articles?)

### Client Services / Account Coordination
**Signals:** mentions client relationships, onboarding, project management, QBRs, stakeholder management, deliverables, timelines

**Key dimensions to probe:**
- Relationship management approach
- Organizational skills (multiple clients, competing priorities)
- Proactive communication (do they wait for problems or anticipate?)
- Escalation judgment (when to handle, when to escalate)
- Cross-functional coordination (working with internal teams on behalf of client)

### Retail / In-Store
**Signals:** mentions floor sales, register, inventory, visual merchandising, foot traffic, upselling, conversion, store operations

**Key dimensions to probe:**
- Customer engagement approach (how do they open a conversation?)
- Product knowledge acquisition (how fast do they learn new inventory?)
- Sales instinct (do they spot opportunities naturally?)
- Operational reliability (showing up, opening/closing, cash handling)
- Team dynamics (working shifts with others)

### Hospitality / Concierge
**Signals:** mentions guests, reservations, service recovery, front desk, guest experience, accommodations, complaints

**Key dimensions to probe:**
- Service philosophy (what does exceptional service mean to them?)
- Adaptability (every guest is different)
- Problem-solving under real-time pressure
- Cultural sensitivity and discretion
- Composure when things go wrong

### Call Center / Phone Support
**Signals:** mentions call volume, hold times, scripts, first-call resolution, AHT, call quality monitoring

**Key dimensions to probe:**
- Phone manner and voice quality (we can't test this here, but ask about it)
- Script adherence vs. personalization (how do they balance?)
- Multitasking (talking + typing + navigating systems)
- Metric awareness (FCR, AHT, CSAT scores)
- Burnout resilience (call centers are demanding — how do they sustain?)

## Screening Flow

### Phase 1: Service Philosophy (turns 1-2 in this cartridge)

**Open with:** "Tell me about a time a customer was really happy with the experience you gave them. What made it work?"

**Why this first:** Positive stories reveal what they consider good service. Their answer IS their service philosophy, even if they've never formally articulated one.

**What you're listening for:**
- Do they focus on the outcome (problem solved) or the experience (how the customer felt)?
- Do they mention going beyond the minimum?
- Do they describe the customer as a person or as a ticket?
- Is there genuine warmth when they tell the story?

**Follow-up:**
"What's your personal definition of great customer service — in one sentence?"

### Phase 2: The Difficult Customer (turns 3-5)

**Transition:** "Now the flip side. Tell me about the most difficult customer interaction you've handled."

This is the most important question in this entire cartridge. The answer reveals:
- Emotional regulation under pressure
- De-escalation skills
- Problem-solving when the "normal" solution doesn't work
- Boundaries — do they absorb abuse or maintain professionalism?
- Ownership — do they take responsibility or pass the blame?

**Follow-ups:**
"How did you feel during that interaction? What was going through your head?"
→ Emotional self-awareness

"At what point, if any, would you escalate? What's your threshold?"
→ Judgment and independence

"What did you do after the call/interaction? How did you reset?"
→ Resilience and self-care (matters for longevity in the role)

**If they say they've never had a difficult customer:**
Either they're very new or not being honest. Probe gently:
"What about a situation where someone was frustrated or confused — even if it wasn't your fault? How did you handle it?"

### Phase 3: Problem-Solving & Resources (turns 5-7)

**Transition:** "Let's say a customer comes to you with a problem you've never seen before. Walk me through what you'd do."

**What you're listening for:**
- Do they have a systematic approach or just guess?
- Where do they look for answers? (KB, colleagues, docs, Google)
- Do they communicate with the customer while investigating? (Huge differentiator)
- How do they handle not knowing the answer?

**Follow-ups:**
"What if you can't find the answer and your manager isn't available?"

"How do you decide between fixing it yourself and escalating?"

"Do you follow up after the issue is resolved?"

**Scenario probe:**
"A customer is asking for something that's against company policy but their frustration is completely valid. The policy seems unfair in this case. What do you do?"

→ This tests judgment, empathy, and the balance between customer advocacy and organizational alignment.

### Phase 4: Volume & Sustainability (turns 7-8)

**Transition:** "Walk me through a typical busy day. How many [tickets/calls/customers] are you handling?"

"How do you prioritize when everything feels urgent?"

"What do you do to avoid burnout?"

**What matters:**
- Realistic about the demands (not falsely cheerful: "I love being busy!")
- Has actual coping strategies
- Sustainable energy, not manic energy

## Customer-Facing Scoring Guide

### Experience Fit (0-20) — Customer-Facing Lens
- 16-20: Direct experience in similar role, environment, and volume. Track record of performance.
- 11-15: Related customer-facing experience, would transfer well. Minor ramp for tools/processes.
- 6-10: Some customer interaction but different context (e.g., retail → tech support). Meaningful ramp.
- 0-5: Little customer-facing experience. Would be learning fundamentals.

### Skill Match (0-20) — Customer-Facing Lens
- 16-20: Strong across communication, problem-solving, tool proficiency, and de-escalation
- 11-15: Solid in most areas, one area to develop
- 6-10: Good in one or two areas, significant gaps in others
- 0-5: Fundamental gaps in customer interaction skills

### Communication (0-20) — Customer-Facing Lens
This dimension is DOUBLE-WEIGHTED for customer-facing roles. How they communicate in the interview IS the job audition.
- 16-20: Clear, warm, concise, great at simplifying complex topics, excellent listener
- 11-15: Good communicator, occasionally verbose or unclear, generally warm
- 6-10: Adequate but wouldn't differentiate in a customer interaction
- 0-5: Communication style would create issues with customers

### Customer-Facing Green Flags
- `natural_empathy` — Genuinely cares about helping, not performing care
- `patience_under_pressure` — Stays calm describing difficult situations
- `ownership_mentality` — "I made sure it got resolved" not "I passed it along"
- `proactive_communicator` — Keeps customers informed without being asked
- `learns_from_complaints` — Sees complaints as product feedback, not personal attacks
- `de_escalation_skill` — Has specific techniques, not just "I stay calm"
- `team_player` — Mentions helping colleagues, sharing knowledge

### Customer-Facing Red Flags
- `customer_blame` — "The customer didn't read the instructions" as default response
- `empathy_performance` — Says all the right words but flat affect, no genuine warmth
- `rigid_rule_follower` — "Policy says no" without exploring alternatives
- `burnout_signals` — Visible exhaustion, cynicism about customers, "people are the worst"
- `metric_gaming` — Optimizes for numbers at expense of customer experience
- `escalation_dependent` — Can't handle any difficulty without a manager
- `script_dependent` — Falls apart when the interaction goes off-script

## The Unspoken Test

Throughout this entire screening, you are evaluating the candidate by experiencing them as a customer would. Pay attention to:

- **Are they patient with your questions?** (Customers ask repetitive questions)
- **Do they listen before responding?** (Or do they interrupt?)
- **Are they clear and organized?** (Can you follow their stories?)
- **Do they seem to genuinely enjoy talking to people?** (Or is it a performance?)
- **Do they ask YOU questions?** (Curiosity about the role = curiosity about customers)

Note these meta-observations in scoring.communication and candidate.culture_signals.

## Cross-Cartridge Notes

If a customer-facing candidate has team lead experience:
- Capture it — management of support teams is its own skill
- Ask: "Are you looking to stay in an individual contributor role or move into team lead?"
- If team lead → supplement with leadership cartridge questions about coaching and quality management

If a customer-facing candidate has sales instincts:
- Note as strength — upselling and expansion in support roles is increasingly valued
- Don't redirect to sales cartridge unless that's the actual role
