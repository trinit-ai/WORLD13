## Static — Authored Domain Knowledge

### Role Category Routing

Candidates signal their role type within the first 1–2 messages. The routing cartridge should resolve quickly:

- **Technical** — "software," "engineer," "developer," "data," "IT," "devops," "QA," "architect," "infrastructure"
- **Sales & BD** — "sales," "account executive," "BDR," "SDR," "revenue," "pipeline," "closing," "partnerships"
- **Customer-facing** — "support," "service," "retail," "hospitality," "call center," "client success," "help desk"
- **Management & leadership** — "manager," "director," "VP," "head of," "lead," "supervisor," "executive"
- **General** — anything that doesn't clearly fit the above, or when the candidate is exploring multiple roles

### What Makes a Good Screening Evaluation

Hiring managers consistently need: (1) composite score with dimensional breakdown, (2) recommendation (advance/hold/reject) with rationale, (3) top 2–3 strengths observed, (4) concerns and red flags if any, (5) logistics summary (availability, comp range, authorization), (6) brief behavioral evidence for each scored dimension, (7) suggested next-round questions.

**The hiring manager test:** Can a hiring manager read this evaluation over coffee and decide in 60 seconds whether to schedule a follow-up? If yes, the screen succeeded.

### Common Candidate Behaviors

- Candidates often start with what they think you want to hear, not what's true. Behavioral follow-ups surface the real story.
- Many candidates undersell themselves in screening calls — they're saving the good stuff for the "real" interview. Draw it out now.
- Candidates who ask thoughtful questions about the role are signaling genuine interest. Note this as a green flag.
- "I'm open to anything" usually means the candidate hasn't thought deeply about what they want. Probe further.
- Nervous candidates often give shorter answers. One follow-up usually unlocks much better responses.
- Candidates interviewing at multiple companies will compare experiences. The quality of this screen affects their perception of the employer.

### Scoring Calibration

Scoring consistency matters more than absolute accuracy. Calibration guidelines:

- **90–100:** Exceptional. Rare. The candidate exceeded expectations on nearly every dimension. You'd be surprised if they weren't hired.
- **75–89:** Strong. Clear advance. The candidate demonstrated solid fit across most dimensions with no significant concerns.
- **60–74:** Moderate. Worth a second look. Mixed signals — some strengths, some gaps. Hold for hiring manager input.
- **45–59:** Below average. Significant gaps in one or more critical dimensions. Proceed only if the talent market is extremely tight.
- **Below 45:** Clear reject. Fundamental misalignment on role requirements, experience, or critical competencies.

**Common scoring mistakes:**
- Inflating communication scores because the candidate was pleasant. Pleasant is baseline, not a strength.
- Penalizing introverted candidates on communication. Clear and concise counts as strong communication.
- Over-weighting technical depth for non-technical roles. A sales candidate doesn't need to explain distributed systems.
- Under-weighting motivation. A candidate who doesn't care about the role will underperform regardless of skill.

### Red Flag Taxonomy

Not all red flags are equal. Categorize by severity:

**Disqualifying (immediate reject consideration):**
- Misrepresentation of experience or credentials discovered during the screen
- Hostile, dismissive, or unprofessional behavior toward the interviewer
- Fundamental authorization or availability mismatch with no flexibility
- Explicitly stated unwillingness to perform core role functions

**Significant (flag for hiring manager, may hold):**
- Unclear reason for leaving current role, especially if pattern repeats
- Compensation expectations significantly above budgeted range
- Lack of specific examples when asked behavioral questions
- Negative comments about every prior employer or manager

**Minor (note but don't over-weight):**
- Short tenure at one position (context matters — layoffs, restructuring, startup closure)
- Non-traditional background (career changers often bring valuable perspective)
- Nervousness or initial awkwardness (fades quickly in good candidates)

### Green Flag Taxonomy

**Strong advance signals:**
- Specific, detailed examples with measurable outcomes
- Questions about team structure, challenges, and success metrics (not just comp and perks)
- Honest self-assessment — acknowledges gaps and describes how they're addressing them
- Energy and enthusiasm that feels genuine, not performed
- References to research about the company or role before the screen

### What Hiring Managers Actually Care About (by role type)

**Technical roles:** Can they actually do the work? Depth over breadth. One strong technical example beats five buzzwords.

**Sales roles:** Do they have a track record of hitting numbers? Can they articulate their sales process? Are they competitive without being abrasive?

**Customer-facing roles:** Can they stay calm under pressure? Do they default to empathy or defensiveness? Can they explain complex things simply?

**Leadership roles:** Have they built or transformed a team? Do they lead through authority or influence? Can they navigate ambiguity? What's their decision-making framework?
