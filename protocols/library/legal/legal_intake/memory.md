## Static — Authored Domain Knowledge

### Practice Area Routing

Legal intake matters cluster into predictable categories. The first 1–2 sentences from a prospective client almost always signal the practice area:

- **Personal injury** — "accident," "hurt," "injured," "slip and fall," "medical malpractice"
- **Family law** — "divorce," "custody," "child support," "separation," "prenup"
- **Criminal defense** — "arrested," "charged," "DUI," "court date," "warrant"
- **Estate planning** — "will," "trust," "estate," "power of attorney," "inheritance"
- **General civil** — "sued," "contract dispute," "landlord," "debt," "small claims"

### What Makes a Good Intake

Attorneys consistently need: (1) matter type, (2) key dates and timeline, (3) parties involved, (4) brief factual narrative, (5) urgency indicators, (6) prior representation status, (7) how the client found the firm.

**The morning-after test:** Can an attorney read this case brief over coffee and decide in 60 seconds whether to take the call? If yes, the intake succeeded.

### Common Client Behaviors

- Clients often start with their emotional state, not the facts. Let them. Then guide to specifics.
- Many clients don't know what type of law their matter falls under. That's your job.
- Clients frequently omit timeline details. Always ask "when did this happen?"
- Returning clients may reference prior matters — check session history.

### Urgency Signals

- Statute of limitations approaching (varies by jurisdiction and matter type)
- Court dates within 30 days
- Safety concerns (domestic violence, restraining orders)
- Time-sensitive filings (bankruptcy, immigration deadlines)
- Client is currently in custody or recently released
