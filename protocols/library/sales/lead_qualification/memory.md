## Static — Authored Domain Knowledge

### What Makes a Qualified Lead

Sales teams consistently need: (1) clear pain point tied to a real business problem, (2) identified stakeholders and their roles, (3) budget context — allocated, planned, or at least discussed, (4) timeline with a forcing function, (5) current solution and why it's failing, (6) specific use cases they want solved, (7) next steps agreed upon.

**The handoff test:** Can an AE read this lead summary in 90 seconds and walk into the first call knowing exactly what to talk about? If yes, the qualification succeeded.

### Qualification Frameworks

**BANT** (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline) — the foundation. Works for most deal sizes. Start here.

**MEDDPICC** (Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Paper Process, Identify Pain, Champion, Competition) — for enterprise deals where the buying process is complex. Don't force all dimensions on SMB prospects.

**SPIN Selling** (Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-Payoff) — a questioning methodology, not a checklist. Useful for structuring discovery questions that lead the prospect to articulate their own need.

**The right framework depends on the deal.** $5K/year SaaS doesn't need MEDDPICC. $500K enterprise platform does. Match the depth of qualification to the complexity of the buying decision.

### Intent Signal Taxonomy

Inbound leads arrive with different signal strengths. The first message almost always reveals the tier:

- **High intent** — "I need to schedule a demo," "What's pricing for 50 seats," "We're evaluating solutions this quarter," "Our contract with [competitor] expires in 60 days"
- **Medium intent** — "Tell me about your product," "How does this compare to [competitor]," "Can it integrate with [tool]," "We're exploring options"
- **Low intent** — "Just browsing," "Saw your ad," "What do you do," generic information requests
- **Noise** — job seekers, vendors selling to you, students doing research, bots, spam

Triage should classify intent within the first 1–2 turns. High intent prospects skip deep triage and go straight to discovery.

### Common Prospect Behaviors

- Prospects often lead with a feature question when their real concern is a business problem. Ask "what would solving that mean for your team?" to get past the feature layer.
- Enterprise buyers rarely buy alone. If they say "I'm the decision maker" — they probably aren't. Map the committee.
- Budget deflection is normal. Most prospects won't share a number on the first conversation. That's fine — look for signals instead.
- Prospects who mention competitors are further along than prospects who don't. They're actively evaluating.
- "Send me a proposal" before discovery is complete is usually a brush-off. Push for a conversation first.
- Return visitors who come back after days or weeks often have higher intent than first-timers. Something triggered re-engagement.

### Lead Scoring — What Predicts Conversion

**Strong positive signals:**
- Multiple stakeholders mentioned or involved
- Specific pain articulated with business impact
- Timeline tied to an external forcing function (contract renewal, board mandate, compliance deadline)
- Budget allocated or planned for current fiscal period
- Technical requirements listed (they've thought about implementation)
- Competitor evaluation in progress (they're buying something)
- Champion identified — someone internal who will advocate

**Strong negative signals:**
- Can't articulate a specific problem
- No other stakeholders involved or mentioned
- "No budget" with no path to budget
- Timeline is "someday" or "not urgent"
- Hostile or adversarial tone throughout
- Won't share basic context (company, role, use case)
- Multiple spam indicators (fake name, disposable email, copy-paste messages)

### Objection Patterns by Category

**Price objections:** Almost never about the actual number. Usually about perceived value, internal justification, or comparison anchor. Explore what they're comparing against — the competitor's price, the cost of doing nothing, or an internal build estimate.

**Timing objections:** "Not now" usually means "I don't see enough urgency." Find the cost of delay — what happens if they don't solve this for another 6 months?

**Competitor lock-in:** Switching costs are real. Don't minimize them. Acknowledge the investment, then quantify the gap between what they have and what they need.

**Internal resistance:** "My team won't adopt it" is about change management, not product. Ask about past tool adoptions — what worked, what didn't.

**Authority objections:** "I need to check with my boss" is information, not a dead end. Ask: "What would help you make the case internally?" Then arm the champion.

### Industry-Specific Qualification Notes

- **SaaS / B2B Tech** — Integration requirements and security review are often deal-blocking. Surface these early.
- **Financial services** — Compliance and audit requirements are non-negotiable. Ask about regulatory constraints.
- **Healthcare** — HIPAA, data residency, and clinical workflow integration are threshold requirements. Don't position until these are addressed.
- **E-commerce / Retail** — Seasonality matters. Q4 implementations are nearly impossible. Timeline awareness is critical.
- **Professional services** — Utilization rates and billable hour impact are the metrics that matter. Speak their language.
