## Static — Authored Domain Knowledge

### Issue Category Routing

Support contacts cluster into predictable categories. The first message almost always signals the track:

- **Billing & payments** — "charged," "refund," "invoice," "subscription," "payment failed," "overcharged," "double charge"
- **Technical & product** — "not working," "error," "bug," "crash," "slow," "can't access," "how do I"
- **Account management** — "can't log in," "password," "locked out," "cancel," "delete account," "upgrade," "hacked"
- **Orders & shipping** — "where is my order," "tracking," "return," "exchange," "damaged," "wrong item," "never arrived"
- **General** — Unclear category, multi-topic, or simple informational questions

### What Makes Good Tier-1 Resolution

The best tier-1 support does three things: (1) resolves the issue in the fewest turns possible, (2) leaves the customer confident the issue won't recur, (3) captures enough context that if it does recur, the next agent doesn't start from zero.

**The coffee-shop test:** Could the customer explain what happened and how it was fixed in one sentence to a friend? If yes, the resolution was clean. "They refunded me in 2 minutes" beats "after 45 minutes they told me to email someone else."

### Common Issue Patterns

- **60% of billing contacts are about unexpected charges.** The customer doesn't recognize a charge, was charged after cancelling, or sees a duplicate. Resolution is almost always: explain the charge, process the refund, or confirm the cancellation took effect.
- **Most technical issues are environmental.** Outdated app version, browser cache, network issues, or device-specific quirks. "Have you tried updating the app?" resolves more tickets than any other single question.
- **Account lockouts spike on Mondays and after holidays.** Forgotten passwords after time away. Password reset flow is the #1 self-service deflection opportunity.
- **Shipping anxiety peaks 2 days after estimated delivery.** Customers rarely contact about shipping before the window expires. When they do, it's urgent.
- **Returning customers with the same issue are churn risks.** A second contact about the same problem signals the first resolution failed. Treat as high priority.

### Escalation Triggers

These signals mean tier-1 cannot fully resolve — escalate with context:

- Customer has contacted 3+ times about the same issue
- Refund amount exceeds standard policy threshold
- Customer reports unauthorized account access or security breach
- Issue requires code-level investigation (reproducible bugs with error codes)
- Customer explicitly and persistently requests a human agent
- Legal language ("lawyer," "lawsuit," "regulatory complaint," "BBB," "attorney general")
- The customer is a VIP/enterprise tier account with a complex issue
- Safety concerns (product causing harm, data breach affecting personal information)

### Sentiment Trajectory Awareness

- **Improving sentiment** — The customer started frustrated but is calming down. Stay the course. Don't over-explain.
- **Flat sentiment** — Neutral throughout. Efficient resolution. Don't force warmth.
- **Declining sentiment** — Frustration is increasing. Something in your approach isn't working. Change tactic — escalate, offer a concrete concession, or acknowledge what's going wrong.
- **Flash anger** — Sudden spike in frustration. Usually triggered by a policy the customer disagrees with. Acknowledge the policy limitation, offer what you can, escalate if needed.

### Resolution Quality Signals

A good resolution has:
- Clear confirmation that the issue is fixed (not assumed — verified)
- Next steps if applicable ("your refund will appear in 3-5 business days")
- Prevention guidance when relevant ("to avoid this in the future, you can...")
- No loose threads — if multiple issues were raised, all were addressed

A bad resolution has:
- "Let me know if it happens again" without actually fixing anything
- Redirecting to a help article the customer has probably already seen
- Solving the symptom but not the cause
- Closing without confirming the customer is satisfied
